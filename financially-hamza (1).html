Topic: Pricing as a finance discipline
01
Step 1 of 7 ¬∑ Foundation
Understand Your Cost Structure
Before you set a single price, you need to know exactly what it costs to deliver.
+
Why this comes first
Most businesses price based on what feels right or what competitors charge. Before any pricing decision, you need four numbers clear. If you can't answer all four, your pricing is based on assumption ‚Äî not analysis.
The four foundations
Variable Costs
Change directly with output. Raw materials, commissions, delivery, transaction fees.
Changes per unit sold
Fixed Costs
Constant regardless of volume. Rent, salaried staff, software, insurance.
Does not change with volume
Contribution Margin
Revenue minus variable costs. What each unit contributes to covering fixed costs.
Revenue ‚àí Variable Cost
Break-Even Point
Minimum volume or revenue to cover all costs. Below this, every sale is a loss.
Fixed Costs √∑ Contribution Margin
Real example ‚Äî Logistics company
üöö 12-route regional logistics company
Quoted jobs on fuel and base salary only. Driver overtime sat in overheads, never allocated per route. Every contract showed +¬£400 margin. When overtime, vehicle wear, and admin were properly allocated ‚Äî actual margin was ‚àí¬£180 per route. Every single contract was losing money. They only discovered this by building a cost-per-job model.
The principle
Every cost must be allocated to the job that caused it. Overhead is a pricing problem you haven't seen yet.
02
Step 2 of 7 ¬∑ Strategy
Define Your Market Positioning
Your strategy sets the ceiling and floor of your pricing before you name a number.
+
Three positions. Three different price logics.
Each positioning strategy carries different pricing logic. Mixing them creates margin problems that look financial but are actually strategic.
üíé Premium
Price signals quality. Customers expect exclusivity and superior outcomes.
No aggressive discounts
‚öñÔ∏è Mid-Market
Competitive price with clear differentiation. Volume and relationship matter.
Selective promotions
üè≠ Cost Leader
Lowest sustainable price. Margins depend entirely on efficiency and volume.
Efficiency is survival
Real example ‚Äî Accounting firm
üìã 18-staff mid-size accounting firm
Pitching general services to everyone, losing 60% of proposals. Every time they lost ‚Äî they cut fees. The problem wasn't the price. It was the position. They repositioned as specialists in construction and real estate only. Fees went up 40%. Win rate went from 40% to 71% in 8 months. Clients stopped comparing them to cheaper generalists because the comparison no longer made sense.
The principle
Strategy defines the boundaries of your pricing. Pricing cannot fix a positioning problem ‚Äî it only makes it more expensive.
03
Step 3 of 7 ¬∑ Demand
Measure Customer Value
The gap between your price and the value you deliver is uncaptured margin.
+
Four things that drive willingness to pay
‚ú® Perceived Benefit
The outcome the customer believes they get ‚Äî not the features. Higher perceived outcome = higher ceiling.
üîÄ Available Alternatives
More viable alternatives lower the ceiling. Fewer substitutes give you more pricing power.
‚ö° Urgency
A customer solving an urgent problem pays more than one making a discretionary purchase.
üîí Switching Cost
The higher the cost to leave ‚Äî in time, money, or risk ‚Äî the less price-sensitive your customer is.
Real example ‚Äî Software reseller
üíª B2B software reseller ‚Äî underpriced by 4x
Pricing at cost plus 20%. A client offhandedly mentioned the software saved their team 3 full days per month. At ¬£350/day per employee that's ¬£12,600/year in value. They were charging ¬£2,200. That's ¬£10,400 per client in uncaptured margin. Multiplied across 40 clients ‚Äî ¬£416,000 a year left on the table. They introduced outcome-based pricing. Average contract value doubled.
The principle
Cost sets the floor. Value sets the ceiling. The commercial opportunity lives in the space between the two.
04
Step 4 of 7 ¬∑ Testing
Test Price Sensitivity
A price change is a business experiment. Simulate the impact before making the decision.
+
The question to answer before any price move
If price increases 5% ‚Äî how much volume drops, and does total profit go up or down? Run three scenarios across revenue, margin, and profit before committing.

Real example ‚Äî Real estate agency
üè¢ 3-office commercial real estate agency
Wanted to raise commission from 2% to 2.5%. Before rolling firm-wide, tested one city for 90 days. Lost 2 clients, kept 11. Net revenue up 22%. The clients who left were the lowest-value accounts. Rolled the change across all three offices in month four. The test made the decision obvious without risking the whole business on a guess.
The principle
Without elasticity analysis, pricing decisions are informed guesses. The simulation turns strategy into a numbers conversation.
05
Step 5 of 7 ¬∑ Control
Control Discount Governance
Every uncontrolled discount erodes the strategy built in steps 1‚Äì4.
+
Three rules that protect margin
Approval Thresholds
Define who approves what level. Rep, manager, CFO ‚Äî each has a ceiling. No exceptions.
Volume Justification
Every discount tied to a volume commitment or contract term. No commitment ‚Äî no discount.
Margin Floor
Every deal must remain above the contribution margin floor. Non-negotiable regardless of discount.
Real example ‚Äî Manufacturing supplier
üè≠ 8-rep manufacturing supplier
Sales reps discounting 15‚Äì25% with no approval process, no volume requirement, no floor. Gross margin dropped 9 points in one quarter. Introduced a 3-tier policy. Margin recovered 8 points the following quarter. Zero deals were lost due to the new policy ‚Äî volume held completely steady.
The principle
A discount without a rule is margin leaving the business. Governance doesn't kill deals ‚Äî it protects the ones worth keeping.
06
Step 6 of 7 ¬∑ Discipline
Monitor & Adjust
Pricing is not a one-time decision. Markets shift, costs change, clients behave differently.
+
Four signals to track every reporting cycle
üìä Margin by Product
Is each product still delivering its target contribution margin? Erosion here is the first warning sign.
‚ö° Sales Velocity
Is volume accelerating or slowing? Unexpected velocity changes mean price is misaligned with market.
üö™ Customer Churn
Price-driven churn means perceived value no longer justifies cost. Track by segment, not total.
üèÅ Competitive Response
A competitor's price move changes your relative positioning even when you don't move at all.
Real example ‚Äî Staffing agency
üë• 11-client staffing agency
Three years of revenue reporting. No margin-by-client report ‚Äî ever. One client: 30% of revenue, 4% contribution margin. Two smaller clients: 32% of revenue, 29% average margin. Repriced Client A with a 22% increase backed by cost data. Client accepted 60%. Agency exited low-margin volume. Revenue fell 8%. Profit grew 19%.
The principle
Adjustment is part of the discipline. A pricing strategy that never gets reviewed is an assumption left running on autopilot.
07
Step 7 of 7 ¬∑ Final Level
Align Pricing with Capital Allocation
This is where pricing becomes a strategic finance decision ‚Äî connecting directly to shareholder value.
+
Three financial outcomes driven by pricing power
üíµ Free Cash Flow
Strong pricing margins generate operating cash that funds growth without external financing.
üìà ROIC
Margin compression lowers return on invested capital. Pricing discipline protects the return that justifies deployment.
üèóÔ∏è Expansion
Products or markets with weak pricing power should receive less capital ‚Äî or none at all.
Real example ‚Äî Retail chain
üè™ 6-branch retail chain
Splitting capex equally across all 6 branches. Two city-centre branches had 28% contribution margin. Four suburban branches had 6% ‚Äî constant discounting to hold volume. Redirected 80% of capex to the two strong branches. One weak branch closed in 18 months. Overall group margin improved from 11% to 19% over two years. The capital decision became obvious once they followed the pricing power ‚Äî not the revenue.
The principle
Capital should follow pricing power ‚Äî not revenue. Where you cannot defend a price, you cannot justify reinvestment.

+
